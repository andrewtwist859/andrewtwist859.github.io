From landfill to the lightness of being

In my desk I have an old iPod.

It's covered in scratches and scuffs from its noble years of service and my lack of care. For a long time I wouldn't dream of leaving the house without it.

Keys. Wallet. Phone. iPod. Headphones.

Now it sits like an artefact of a strange cultural waste management experiment. Several years of history and 80gb of landfill indie compressed onto a 46mm hard drive.

Almost every song revealed by its click wheel is available on Spotify now. My old iPod is redundant in almost every sense - but I can't bring myself to throw it away.

From time to time, I turn it on, plug in my headphones and head out with it clasped in my hand. I walk around listening only to songs that I chose for the soundtrack to an earlier part of my life.

When powered off this iPod is nothing but an old MP3 player full of 'acquired' (stolen) music. But when switched on, it is a wormhole to the late 2000s and a shortcut back to a cherished time.

I am proud to be part of the landfill indie generation.

I've seen a lot of debate online recently about 'landfill indie' prompted by articles in Vice and NME. I have no interest in an after the fact debate about the merits or shortcomings of music from those years. I have no interest in trying to define or redefine the term 'landfill indie.' I have no interest in whether you use that term in a positive or negative way.

I know what that music, and that period of time, means to me.

And it was the fucking best.

Or at least that is how I choose to remember it. Deep down I know it wasn't that simple. I know there was still anxiety. There were still arguments. There were still nights were we couldn't get in somewhere because someone didn't have ID.

There was still a lot of walking round wondering what they meant in their last message. There was a lot of wondering why they hadn't messaged back since then. A lot of wondering how they felt about you. A lot of wondering what would or would not happen next between you.

There was a lot of loneliness and a lot of tension.

There was a lot of darkness to bleed into the light.

I remember reading a quote at the time in a book I'd bought to try and seem cleverer than I was. It was a book called 'The Unbearable Lightness of Being' by Milan Kundera - and it is a book I have still never read in full.

Early on enough in that book for me to have not lost interest, is the phrase:

"In the sunset of dissolution, everything is illuminated by the aura of nostalgia, even the guillotine."

For some reason, this quote has stuck with me in the 13 years or so since I first read it. At the time I thought it was a nice phrase and that I'd try and rip it off for a lyric or something.

Now, it makes more sense.

I let the aura of nostalgia illuminate everything from that period in my life. Even though I know it wasn't always so perfect and pure.

As I get older I worry that I am going to get trapped in the wormhole. That the nostalgia will blind me. That I will exist only in the embellished memories of a time that I will never be able to relive. That I walk around the landfill polishing every piece of scrap until it shines - from rubble to the ritz, if you will.

I find it hard to remember that there is more life to live and more moments to find the soundtrack for. That there is a still a present and a future, no matter how loud the past speaks to me when I switch on that old iPod.
